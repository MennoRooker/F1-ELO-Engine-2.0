Formula 1 is often refered to as the pinnacle of motor sport with its high-speed action and remarkable combination of strength and finesse needed to operate its single-seater cars. Formula 1 drivers are seen as the best of the best when it comes to finding small optimizations in turns and finding a way to overtake competitors moving at 300+ km/h. However, it would be wrong to suggest that Formula 1 is a one-man show.

Behind every points earned is a team of dozens of strategists, engineers and analysts whose job it is to gain an inch anywhere they can. F1 is as much a motorsport as it is a huge engineering endeavor. So much so that much of the success of certain drivers is attributed to their team rather than to them.

"Races are won at the track. Championships are won at the factory." -Mercedes (2019)

While many people can appreciate the technical intricacies of building what is essentialy a rocketship with wheels, others simply want to know who is truly the best driver on the grid. Any discussion about the best drivers in the world seems to inevitably turn into an argument about the importance of having the best car, which is where this leaderboard comes in!

Here below is an attempt to quantify this importance by applying penalties to the drivers whose teams are leading the constructors standings. This way a driver with good machinery needs to score significantly more points than his competitors if he wants to stay on top. The penalties are calculated dynamically throughout a season and applied at the start of a race (not the first race of the season since there are no standings yet), these penalties are deducted from the points they earn during the race.

Constructors:

Standing Penalties
1st -10
2nd -9
3rd -8
4th -7
5th -6
6th -5
7th -4
8th -3
9th -2

For consistency reasons the post-2010 scoring system is applied to all seasons and any points earned from the fastest lap are removed. The dataset used consists of information from 1950 to 2024.
